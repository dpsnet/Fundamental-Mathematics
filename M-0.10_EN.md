## 2. Rigorous Theory of Hausdorff Measures

### 2.1 Construction of Hausdorff Measures

**Definition 2.1.1** Let $(X, d)$ be a metric space and $F \subseteq X$ be an arbitrary subset. For $s \geq 0$ and $\delta > 0$, define the **$\delta$-approximate Hausdorff measure** as:

$$\mathcal{H}^s_\delta(F) = \inf\left\{\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} |U_i|^s : \{U_i\}_{i=1}^{\infty} \text{ is a } \delta\text{-cover of } F\right\}$$

where $|U_i| = \sup\{d(x,y) : x, y \in U_i\}$ denotes the diameter of set $U_i$.

**Definition 2.1.2** The **$s$-dimensional Hausdorff measure** is defined as:

$$\mathcal{H}^s(F) = \lim_{\delta \to 0^+} \mathcal{H}^s_\delta(F) = \sup_{\delta > 0} \mathcal{H}^s_\delta(F)$$

**Theorem 2.1.1** (Well-definedness of Hausdorff measure) The above limit exists (possibly $+\infty$), and $\mathcal{H}^s$ is a Borel regular outer measure on $X$.

**Proof:**

(1) **Existence of limit:** For $0 < \delta_1 < \delta_2$, any $\delta_1$-cover is also a $\delta_2$-cover, therefore:
$$\mathcal{H}^s_{\delta_1}(F) \geq \mathcal{H}^s_{\delta_2}(F)$$
This shows that $\mathcal{H}^s_\delta(F)$ is monotonically decreasing with respect to $\delta$, hence the limit exists.

(2) **Outer measure properties:**
- **Non-negativity:** Clearly $\mathcal{H}^s(F) \geq 0$, and $\mathcal{H}^s(\emptyset) = 0$
- **Monotonicity:** If $E \subseteq F$, then any cover of $F$ is also a cover of $E$, hence $\mathcal{H}^s(E) \leq \mathcal{H}^s(F)$
- **Countable subadditivity:** Let $\{F_i\}_{i=1}^{\infty}$ be a countable family of sets. For any $\epsilon > 0$ and $\delta > 0$, for each $F_i$ select a $\delta$-cover $\{U_{ij}\}_j$ such that:
  $$\sum_j |U_{ij}|^s \leq \mathcal{H}^s_\delta(F_i) + \frac{\epsilon}{2^i}$$
  Then $\{U_{ij}\}_{i,j}$ is a $\delta$-cover of $\bigcup_i F_i$, and:
  $$\mathcal{H}^s_\delta\left(\bigcup_i F_i\right) \leq \sum_{i,j} |U_{ij}|^s \leq \sum_i \mathcal{H}^s_\delta(F_i) + \epsilon$$
  Let $\delta \to 0$ and $\epsilon \to 0$ to obtain countable subadditivity.

(3) **Borel regularity:** Standard proof omitted, see Falconer "The Mathematical Foundations of Fractal Geometry".

**Theorem 2.1.2** (Critical property of measures) There exists a unique critical value $s_0 \geq 0$ such that:
$$\mathcal{H}^s(F) = \begin{cases} +\infty, & s < s_0 \\ 0, & s > s_0 \end{cases}$$

and $s_0 = \dim_H(F)$ (Hausdorff dimension).

**Proof:**

(1) Let $s < t$, $|U_i| \leq \delta < 1$, then $|U_i|^t \leq \delta^{t-s} |U_i|^s$, therefore:
$$\mathcal{H}^t_\delta(F) \leq \delta^{t-s} \mathcal{H}^s_\delta(F)$$

(2) If $\mathcal{H}^s(F) < \infty$, then when $t > s$:
$$\mathcal{H}^t(F) \leq \lim_{\delta \to 0} \delta^{t-s} \mathcal{H}^s(F) = 0$$

(3) If $\mathcal{H}^t(F) > 0$, then when $s < t$, $\mathcal{H}^s(F) = +\infty$.

Therefore the critical value exists, and by definition of Hausdorff dimension, $s_0 = \dim_H(F)$.

### 2.2 Basic Properties of Hausdorff Measures

**Theorem 2.2.1** (Scaling property) Let $F \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$, $\lambda > 0$, define $\lambda F = \{\lambda x : x \in F\}$, then:
$$\mathcal{H}^s(\lambda F) = \lambda^s \mathcal{H}^s(F)$$

**Proof:** If $\{U_i\}$ is a $\delta$-cover of $F$, then $\{\lambda U_i\}$ is a $\lambda\delta$-cover of $\lambda F$, and $|\lambda U_i| = \lambda |U_i|$. Therefore:
$$\mathcal{H}^s_{\lambda\delta}(\lambda F) \leq \sum_i |\lambda U_i|^s = \lambda^s \sum_i |U_i|^s$$
Taking infimum and limit gives $\mathcal{H}^s(\lambda F) \leq \lambda^s \mathcal{H}^s(F)$. The reverse inequality can be proved similarly.

**Theorem 2.2.2** (Measure control under Lipschitz mappings) Let $f: X \to Y$ be a Lipschitz mapping with Lipschitz constant $L$, then for any $F \subseteq X$:
$$\mathcal{H}^s(f(F)) \leq L^s \mathcal{H}^s(F)$$

**Proof:** If $\{U_i\}$ is a $\delta$-cover of $F$, then $\{f(U_i)\}$ is a $L\delta$-cover of $f(F)$, and $|f(U_i)| \leq L|U_i|$. Therefore:
$$\mathcal{H}^s_{L\delta}(f(F)) \leq \sum_i |f(U_i)|^s \leq L^s \sum_i |U_i|^s$$
Taking infimum and limit yields the conclusion.

**Corollary 2.2.1** (Geometric invariance of Hausdorff measure) Hausdorff measure is invariant under isometric transformations.

**Theorem 2.2.3** (Countable stability) For any countable family $\{F_i\}_{i=1}^{\infty}$:
$$\mathcal{H}^s\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} F_i\right) = \sup_i \mathcal{H}^s(F_i)$$
holds when $s = \dim_H(\bigcup_i F_i)$ and the dimensions of each $F_i$ are different.

### 2.3 Computational Methods for Hausdorff Measures

**Theorem 2.3.1** (Mass distribution principle) Let $\mu$ be a Borel probability measure on $F$. If there exist $s > 0$ and $c > 0$ such that for all Borel sets $U$ with $|U| \leq r$:
$$\mu(U) \leq c |U|^s$$
then $\mathcal{H}^s(F) \geq \frac{1}{c} > 0$, and thus $\dim_H(F) \geq s$.

**Proof:** Let $\{U_i\}$ be an arbitrary $\delta$-cover of $F$, then:
$$1 = \mu(F) \leq \sum_i \mu(U_i) \leq c \sum_i |U_i|^s$$
Therefore $\sum_i |U_i|^s \geq \frac{1}{c}$, taking infimum gives $\mathcal{H}^s_\delta(F) \geq \frac{1}{c}$, letting $\delta \to 0$ yields the result.

**Theorem 2.3.2** (Frostman's lemma) Let $F \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ be a Borel set. $\mathcal{H}^s(F) > 0$ if and only if there exists a Borel probability measure $\mu$ on $F$ and constant $c > 0$ such that:
$$\mu(B(x,r)) \leq c r^s$$
for all $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and $r > 0$.

**Proof outline:**
- **Sufficiency:** Follows directly from the mass distribution principle.
- **Necessity:** Uses Hahn-Banach theorem and Riesz representation theorem to construct measure, see Mattila "Geometry of Sets and Measures".

### 2.4 Relationship Between Hausdorff Measures and Hausdorff Dimension

**Theorem 2.4.1** (Measure-theoretic characterization of dimension) For any $F \subseteq X$:
$$\dim_H(F) = \inf\{s : \mathcal{H}^s(F) = 0\} = \sup\{s : \mathcal{H}^s(F) = +\infty\}$$

**Theorem 2.4.2** (Positive finite measure and dimension) If $0 < \mathcal{H}^s(F) < \infty$, then $s = \dim_H(F)$, and $F$ is called an **$s$-set**.

**Definition 2.4.1** A set $F$ is called an **$s$-set** if $0 < \mathcal{H}^s(F) < \infty$.

**Theorem 2.4.3** (Measurability of s-sets) Any $s$-set can be expressed as the union of countably many $s$-sets and a $\mathcal{H}^s$-null set.

## 3. Dual Theory of Packing Measures

### 3.1 Definition of Packing Measure

Hausdorff measure is based on the concept of "covering", while packing measure is based on its dual concept "packing".

**Definition 3.1.1** Let $(X, d)$ be a metric space and $F \subseteq X$. A **packing** of $F$ is a family of disjoint balls $\{B(x_i, r_i)\}_{i \in I}$ with centers in $F$, where $x_i \in F$.

**Definition 3.1.2** For $s \geq 0$ and $\delta > 0$, define the **$\delta$-approximate packing measure**:
$$\mathcal{P}^s_\delta(F) = \sup\left\{\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} (2r_i)^s : \{B(x_i, r_i)\} \text{ is a packing of } F, x_i \in F, r_i \leq \delta\right\}$$

**Definition 3.1.3** The **$s$-dimensional packing pre-measure** is defined as:
$$\mathcal{P}^s_0(F) = \inf_{\delta > 0} \mathcal{P}^s_\delta(F) = \lim_{\delta \to 0^+} \mathcal{P}^s_\delta(F)$$

**Note:** $\mathcal{P}^s_0$ is generally not an outer measure (does not satisfy countable subadditivity) and requires modification.

**Definition 3.1.4** The **$s$-dimensional packing measure** is defined as:
$$\mathcal{P}^s(F) = \inf\left\{\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \mathcal{P}^s_0(F_i) : F \subseteq \bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} F_i\right\}$$

That is, the outer measure obtained by applying Carathéodory construction to $\mathcal{P}^s_0$.

### 3.2 Centered Packing Measure

**Definition 3.2.1** For $\delta > 0$, define the **$\delta$-centered packing measure**:
$$\mathcal{C}^s_\delta(F) = \sup\left\{\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} (2r_i)^s : \{B(x_i, r_i)\} \text{ are disjoint}, x_i \in F, r_i \leq \delta\right\}$$

**Definition 3.2.2** The **centered packing pre-measure**:
$$\mathcal{C}^s_0(F) = \lim_{\delta \to 0} \mathcal{C}^s_\delta(F)$$

**Theorem 3.2.1** For all $F \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$:
$$\mathcal{H}^s(F) \leq \mathcal{C}^s_0(F) \leq \mathcal{P}^s(F)$$

**Proof:** The proof is based on the duality relationship between covering and packing; detailed arguments can be found in standard literature.

### 3.3 Packing Measure and Packing Dimension

**Definition 3.3.1** The **Packing dimension** is defined as:
$$\dim_P(F) = \inf\{s : \mathcal{P}^s(F) = 0\} = \sup\{s : \mathcal{P}^s(F) = +\infty\}$$

**Theorem 3.3.1** (Dimension inequality) For any $F \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$:
$$\dim_H(F) \leq \dim_P(F)$$

**Proof:** Follows directly from $\mathcal{H}^s \leq \mathcal{P}^s$.

**Theorem 3.3.2** (Equivalent definition of packing dimension)
$$\dim_P(F) = \inf\left\{\sup_i \dim_P(F_i) : F = \bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} F_i\right\}$$

### 3.4 Measure Duality Theorem

**Theorem 3.4.1** (Measure duality) For any $F \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ and $s \geq 0$:
$$\mathcal{H}^s(F) \leq \mathcal{P}^s(F) \leq 2^s \mathcal{H}^s(F)$$

holds when $F$ is a Borel set.

**Theorem 3.4.2** (Dimension trichotomy) Define **upper box dimension** $\overline{\dim}_B$ and **lower box dimension** $\underline{\dim}_B$, then:
$$\dim_H(F) \leq \underline{\dim}_B(F) \leq \overline{\dim}_B(F)$$
and
$$\dim_H(F) \leq \dim_P(F) \leq \overline{\dim}_B(F)$$

## 4. Variational Principles of Fractal Measures

### 4.1 Formulation of Variational Problems

**Definition 4.1.1** Let $\mathcal{M}(F)$ be the set of all Borel probability measures on $F$. For $\mu \in \mathcal{M}(F)$, define its **$s$-energy** as:
$$I_s(\mu) = \int\int \frac{d\mu(x) d\mu(y)}{|x-y|^s}$$

**Theorem 4.1.1** (Finiteness of energy and dimension) Let $F \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$, then:
$$\dim_H(F) = \sup\{s : \exists \mu \in \mathcal{M}(F), I_s(\mu) < \infty\}$$

**Proof outline:**
- If $I_s(\mu) < \infty$, then $\mu$ satisfies the conditions of the mass distribution principle, hence $\dim_H(F) \geq s$
- Conversely, if $\dim_H(F) > s$, by Frostman's lemma there exists a measure satisfying the conditions

### 4.2 Existence of Extremal Measures

**Theorem 4.2.1** (Existence of extremal measures) Let $F$ be a compact set and $s < \dim_H(F)$, then there exists $\mu \in \mathcal{M}(F)$ such that $I_s(\mu) < \infty$.

**Definition 4.2.1** The **Hausdorff dimension** of measure $\mu$ is defined as:
$$\dim_H(\mu) = \inf\{\dim_H(E) : \mu(E) > 0\}$$

**Theorem 4.2.2** (Variational characterization of measure dimension)
$$\dim_H(\mu) = \sup\{s : I_s(\mu) < \infty\}$$

### 4.3 Variational Principle and Dimension Formula

**Theorem 4.3.1** (Variational principle for dimension)
$$\dim_H(F) = \sup_{\mu \in \mathcal{M}(F)} \dim_H(\mu)$$

**Theorem 4.3.2** (Variational principle for multifractals) Let $f(\alpha)$ be the multifractal spectrum, then:
$$f(\alpha) = \inf_{q \in \mathbb{R}} (\alpha q - \tau(q))$$
where $\tau(q)$ is the mass exponent function.

## 5. Multifractal Measure Theory

### 5.1 Definition of Multifractals

**Definition 5.1.1** Let $\mu$ be a Borel measure on $\mathbb{R}^n$. The **pointwise dimension** (or local dimension) at point $x$ is defined as:
$$\alpha(x) = \lim_{r \to 0} \frac{\log \mu(B(x,r))}{\log r}$$
(when the limit exists)

**Definition 5.1.2** For $\alpha \geq 0$, define the **level set**:
$$E_\alpha = \{x : \alpha(x) = \alpha\}$$

**Definition 5.1.3** The **multifractal spectrum** is defined as:
$$f(\alpha) = \dim_H(E_\alpha)$$

### 5.2 Multifractal Spectrum

**Definition 5.2.1** The **mass exponent function** $\tau(q)$ is defined as:
$$\tau(q) = \lim_{r \to 0} \frac{\log \sum_i \mu(B_i)^q}{\log r}$$
where $\{B_i\}$ is any $r$-mesh partition.

**Theorem 5.2.1** (Legendre transform relation) Under appropriate conditions:
$$f(\alpha) = \inf_q (\alpha q - \tau(q))$$
$$\tau(q) = \inf_\alpha (\alpha q - f(\alpha))$$

**Proof outline:** Based on large deviation theory, see section below.

### 5.3 Large Deviation Theory Framework

**Theorem 5.3.1** (Gärtner-Ellis type theorem) Let $\tau(q)$ be a convex function differentiable at 0, then:
$$f(\alpha) = \inf_q (\alpha q - \tau(q))$$
holds for all $\alpha \in [\tau'(+\infty), \tau'(-\infty)]$.

**Theorem 5.3.2** (Measure-theoretic foundation of multifractals) Define the **coarse-grained spectrum**:
$$\bar{f}(\alpha) = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \lim_{r \to 0} \frac{\log N_r(\alpha, \epsilon)}{-\log r}$$
where $N_r(\alpha, \epsilon)$ is the number of boxes satisfying $r^{\alpha+\epsilon} \leq \mu(B_i) \leq r^{\alpha-\epsilon}$.

Then under appropriate conditions, $\bar{f}(\alpha) = f(\alpha)$.

## 6. Fractal Measures and Spectral Dimension Flow

### 6.1 Measure-Theoretic Definition of Spectral Dimension

In M-0.5 and M-0.7, we introduced the concept of spectral dimension. Now we give a rigorous definition from the measure-theoretic perspective.

**Definition 6.1.1** Let $\mu$ be a Borel measure on metric space $(X, d)$ and $\{T_t\}_{t > 0}$ be the heat semigroup. Define the **heat kernel** $p(t, x, y)$ as the kernel function satisfying:
$$T_t f(x) = \int p(t, x, y) f(y) d\mu(y)$$

**Definition 6.1.2** The **heat kernel trace** is defined as:
$$Z(t) = \int p(t, x, x) d\mu(x) = \text{Tr}(e^{-t\Delta})$$
where $\Delta$ is the Laplacian associated with measure $\mu$.

**Theorem 6.1.1** (Measure-theoretic characterization of spectral dimension) If there exists $d_s > 0$ such that:
$$Z(t) \sim t^{-d_s/2}, \quad t \to 0^+$$
then $d_s$ is called the **spectral dimension**.

### 6.2 Measure Families and Spectral Dimension Flow

**Definition 6.2.1** Let $\{\mu_E\}_{E > 0}$ be a family of measures depending on energy scale $E$, with corresponding spectral dimension $d_s(E)$. If $d_s(E)$ varies with $E$, it is called **spectral dimension flow**.

**Theorem 6.2.1** (Measure-theoretic interpretation of spectral dimension flow) Let the measure family satisfy:
$$\mu_E = \mu \circ \phi_E^{-1}$$
where $\phi_E$ is a scale transformation depending on $E$, then:
$$d_s(E) = d_s(0) \cdot \frac{\log \lambda(E)}{\log E}$$
where $\lambda(E)$ is the scale factor.

**Theorem 6.2.2** (Spectral dimension flow in unified field theory) Under fixed 4-dimensional topology, the spectral dimension flow satisfies:
$$d_s(E) = 4 - \alpha \log^{-1}(E/E_0)$$
where $d_s(E) \to 2$ as $E \to \infty$ and $d_s(E) \to 4$ as $E \to 0$.

### 6.3 Relationship Between Heat Kernel and Measure

**Theorem 6.3.1** (Measure representation of heat kernel) For the natural measure $\mu$ on fractal set $F$:
$$p(t, x, y) = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-\lambda t} dE_\lambda(x, y)$$
where $\{E_\lambda\}$ is the spectral measure.

**Theorem 6.3.2** (Walk dimension and spectral dimension) Define **walk dimension** $d_w$ as the scaling exponent of random walk, then:
$$d_s = \frac{2 d_f}{d_w}$$
where $d_f = \dim_H(F)$ is the Hausdorff dimension of the fractal.

## 7. Fractal Measure Space

### 7.1 Topology of Measure Space

**Definition 7.1.1** Let $\mathcal{M}(X)$ be the set of all finite Radon measures on metric space $X$. **Weak convergence** is defined as:
$$\mu_n \xrightarrow{w} \mu \iff \int f d\mu_n \to \int f d\mu, \quad \forall f \in C_b(X)$$

**Theorem 7.1.1** (Prohorov's theorem) A subset $\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathcal{M}(X)$ is relatively compact if and only if it is tight.

### 7.2 Convergence of Measures

**Definition 7.2.1** The generalization of **Hausdorff metric** to measure space is defined as:
$$d_H(\mu, \nu) = \inf\{\epsilon : \mu(A) \leq \nu(A_\epsilon) + \epsilon, \nu(A) \leq \mu(A_\epsilon) + \epsilon, \forall A\}$$
where $A_\epsilon$ is the $\epsilon$-neighborhood of $A$.

**Theorem 7.2.1** (Measure convergence and dimension continuity) If $\mu_n \to \mu$ and the Hausdorff dimensions of the supports of each $\mu_n$ converge, then:
$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \dim_H(\text{supp}(\mu_n)) = \dim_H(\text{supp}(\mu))$$

### 7.3 Completeness of Measure Space

**Theorem 7.3.1** The measure space $\mathcal{M}(X)$ equipped with the weak topology is a Polish space (when $X$ is a Polish space).

**Theorem 7.3.2** (Continuity of fractal dimension in measure space) The mapping $\mu \mapsto \dim_H(\mu)$ is upper semi-continuous.

## 8. Application Examples

### 8.1 Measure Analysis of Cantor Set

**Example 8.1.1** (Classical Cantor set) Let $C$ be the classical Cantor set. Its Hausdorff measure satisfies:
$$\mathcal{H}^{\log 2/\log 3}(C) = 1$$

**Proof:** The Hausdorff dimension of the Cantor set is $s = \log 2/\log 3$. At the $n$-th step of construction, there are $2^n$ intervals of length $3^{-n}$, with total "$s$-dimensional length":
$$2^n \cdot (3^{-n})^s = 2^n \cdot 2^{-n} = 1$$

**Theorem 8.1.1** (Natural measure on Cantor set) There exists a unique Borel probability measure $\mu$ on $C$ satisfying:
$$\mu(I) = 2^{-n}$$
for each basic interval $I$ at the $n$-th step.

### 8.2 Measures of Sierpinski Gasket

**Example 8.2.1** (Sierpinski gasket) The Hausdorff dimension of Sierpinski gasket $S$ is $s = \log 3/\log 2$, and:
$$0 < \mathcal{H}^s(S) < \infty$$

### 8.3 Measures of Random Fractals

**Theorem 8.3.1** (Measures of random Cantor sets) Let $C_\omega$ be a random Cantor set. Its Hausdorff dimension is almost surely constant $s$, and:
$$\mathbb{E}[\mathcal{H}^s(C_\omega)] > 0$$
