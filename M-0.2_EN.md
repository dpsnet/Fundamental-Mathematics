## 1. Introduction

The cross research between fractal geometry and number theory provides a new geometric perspective for the representation of irrational numbers. In traditional number theory, irrational numbers are usually represented by continued fractions, series expansions, or algebraic equations, but these methods lack intuitive geometric meaning. Fractal geometry provides a geometric structure-based representation method for irrational numbers through the concept of fractal dimension.

This module systematically establishes the basic theoretical framework of fractal dimension, proves that all irrational numbers can be represented as rational combinations of fractal dimensions, laying a solid mathematical foundation for subsequent inner product space theory, unified expression theory, and mapping relationship of Ramanujan formula.

Fractal dimension is a key indicator describing the complexity of fractal sets, among which Hausdorff dimension and box dimension are the two most commonly used definitions [1]. Hausdorff dimension is based on measure theory and has a strict mathematical foundation; box dimension is based on covering methods and is relatively easy to calculate. For self-similar fractal sets, these two dimension definitions are usually equal.

Construction methods of fractal dimension mainly include self-similar fractals and graph-directed fractals. Self-similar fractals are constructed through Iterated Function Systems (IFS) and have clear mathematical properties; graph-directed fractals are constructed through directed graphs and similarity transformations, and can generate more complex fractal sets [2]. By adjusting construction parameters, fractal sets with arbitrary real dimensions can be generated.

The core contribution of this module is to prove that all irrational numbers can be represented as rational combinations of fractal dimensions. This result is based on the denseness and linear independence of fractal dimensions, providing a theoretical foundation for the geometric representation of irrational numbers. Further analysis of the representation differences between algebraic irrational numbers and transcendental irrational numbers proves that the representation of transcendental irrational numbers contains at least one transcendental fractal dimension.

The validity of the theory is verified through specific examples (fractal dimension representation of π and e). Finally, a method for characterizing fractal dimension complexity and the problem of minimum complexity representation are proposed, as well as several open problems, pointing out directions for subsequent research.

## 2. Construction of Fractal Dimension Space

### 2.1 Definition of Fractal Dimension Space

**Definition 2.1.1** Let $\mathcal{D}$ be the set of all fractal dimensions, $\mathbb{Q}$ be the rational number field. Define the fractal dimension space $V$ as the linear span of $\mathcal{D} \cup \mathbb{Q}$ over the rational number field:

$$V = \text{span}_{\mathbb{Q}}(\mathcal{D} \cup \mathbb{Q}) = \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^n q_i d_i + \sum_{j=1}^m r_j \mid q_i, r_j \in \mathbb{Q}, d_i \in \mathcal{D}, n, m \in \mathbb{N} \right\}$$

**Theorem 2.1.1** The fractal dimension space $V$ is a vector space over $\mathbb{Q}$.

**Proof**:

1. **Closure**: For any $x, y \in V$, there exist representations:
   $$x = \sum_{i=1}^{n_x} q_{xi} d_{xi} + \sum_{j=1}^{m_x} r_{xj}$$
   $$y = \sum_{i=1}^{n_y} q_{yi} d_{yi} + \sum_{j=1}^{m_y} r_{yj}$$

   Then $x + y = \sum_{i=1}^{n_x} q_{xi} d_{xi} + \sum_{i=1}^{n_y} q_{yi} d_{yi} + \sum_{j=1}^{m_x} r_{xj} + \sum_{j=1}^{m_y} r_{yj} \in V$

2. **Scalar multiplication closure**: For any $q \in \mathbb{Q}$ and $x \in V$, $qx = \sum_{i=1}^{n_x} (q q_{xi}) d_{xi} + \sum_{j=1}^{m_x} (q r_{xj}) \in V$

3. **Vector space axioms**: It is easy to verify that $V$ satisfies all vector space axioms.

### 2.2 Construction of Inner Product

**Definition 2.2.1** Define the inner product $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle: V \times V \to \mathbb{Q}$ on the fractal dimension space $V$ as follows:

1. For $d_1, d_2 \in \mathcal{D}$, $\langle d_1, d_2 \rangle = \delta_{d_1 d_2}$ (Kronecker $\delta$)
2. For $q_1, q_2 \in \mathbb{Q}$, $\langle q_1, q_2 \rangle = q_1 q_2$
3. For $d \in \mathcal{D}, q \in \mathbb{Q}$, $\langle d, q \rangle = 0$
4. Linear extension to the entire $V$ space

**Definition 2.2.2** The Kronecker $\delta$ function $\delta_{ab}$ is defined as:

$$\delta_{ab} = \begin{cases}
1, & \text{if } a = b \\
0, & \text{if } a \neq b
\end{cases}$$

**Theorem 2.2.1** The above-defined $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ is an inner product on the fractal dimension space $V$.

**Proof**: Need to verify the four properties of inner product:

1. **Positive definiteness**: For any $x \in V$, $\langle x, x \rangle \geq 0$, and $\langle x, x \rangle = 0$ if and only if $x = 0$.

   Let $x = \sum_{i=1}^n q_i d_i + r$, where $d_i \in \mathcal{D}$, $q_i, r \in \mathbb{Q}$, then:
   $$\langle x, x \rangle = \sum_{i=1}^n q_i^2 + r^2 \geq 0$$

   If $\langle x, x \rangle = 0$, then $\sum_{i=1}^n q_i^2 = 0$ and $r^2 = 0$, i.e., $q_i = 0$ ($i=1,2,\ldots,n$) and $r = 0$, thus $x = 0$.

   In particular, if $x$ is a pure rational number, i.e., $x = r$, then $\langle x, x \rangle = r^2 \geq 0$, and $\langle x, x \rangle = 0$ if and only if $r = 0$.

2. **Symmetry**: For any $x, y \in V$, $\langle x, y \rangle = \langle y, x \rangle$.

   Directly from the inner product definition, since rational number multiplication is commutative, and the Kronecker $\delta$ function satisfies $\delta_{ab} = \delta_{ba}$.

3. **Linearity**: For any $x, y, z \in V$ and $q \in \mathbb{Q}$, we have:
   - $\langle x + y, z \rangle = \langle x, z \rangle + \langle y, z \rangle$
   - $\langle qx, y \rangle = q\langle x, y \rangle$

   Directly from the linear structure of the inner product definition.

4. **Conjugate symmetry**: Since $V$ is a real space, conjugate symmetry is equivalent to symmetry, which has been verified.

Therefore, $(V, \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle)$ constitutes an inner product space.

**Corollary 2.2.1** The norm on the fractal dimension space $V$ is defined as:

$$\|x\| = \sqrt{\langle x, x \rangle}$$

which satisfies all norm properties.

## 3. Construction and Strict Proof of Orthogonal Basis

### 3.1 Existence of Linearly Independent Set

**Theorem 3.1.1** There exist fractal dimensions $d_1, d_2 \in \mathcal{D}$ and rational number $q_0 \in \mathbb{Q}$ such that $\{d_1, d_2, q_0\}$ is linearly independent over $\mathbb{Q}$.

**Proof**:

1. From Lemma 1.3.2 of module M-0.1, there exist infinitely many fractal dimensions that are linearly independent over $\mathbb{Q}$. Choose two of them $d_1, d_2$.

2. Choose $q_0 = 1$ (any non-zero rational number works).

3. Suppose there exist $a, b, c \in \mathbb{Q}$ such that $a d_1 + b d_2 + c \cdot 1 = 0$.

4. If $c \neq 0$, then $1 = -\frac{a}{c}d_1 - \frac{b}{c}d_2$, i.e., the rational number 1 can be represented as a rational linear combination of fractal dimensions.

   But fractal dimensions are irrational numbers (in most cases), and their rational linear combinations are still irrational or rational numbers. Since $d_1, d_2$ are linearly independent, $-\frac{a}{c}d_1 - \frac{b}{c}d_2$ is irrational, contradicting the fact that 1 is rational.

5. Therefore $c = 0$, thus $a d_1 + b d_2 = 0$.

6. Since $d_1, d_2$ are linearly independent, we have $a = b = 0$.

7. Therefore $\{d_1, d_2, 1\}$ is linearly independent over $\mathbb{Q}$.

### 3.2 Detailed Derivation of Schmidt Orthogonalization

**Theorem 3.2.1 (Schmidt Orthogonalization Algorithm)** Let $\{v_1, v_2, v_3\}$ be a linearly independent set in the inner product space $V$, then there exists an orthogonal set $\{u_1, u_2, u_3\}$ such that:

$$\text{span}_{\mathbb{Q}}\{v_1, v_2, v_3\} = \text{span}_{\mathbb{Q}}\{u_1, u_2, u_3\}$$

**Proof**: Construct through the Schmidt orthogonalization process:

1. **First step**: $u_1 = v_1$ (keep the first vector unchanged)

2. **Second step**: Calculate $u_2 = v_2 - \text{proj}_{u_1}(v_2)$, where the projection operator is defined as:
   $$\text{proj}_{u}(v) = \frac{\langle v, u \rangle}{\langle u, u \rangle} u$$

   Substituting $u = u_1 = v_1$, $v = v_2$, we get:
   $$u_2 = v_2 - \frac{\langle v_2, v_1 \rangle}{\langle v_1, v_1 \rangle} v_1$$

3. **Third step**: Calculate $u_3 = v_3 - \text{proj}_{u_1}(v_3) - \text{proj}_{u_2}(v_3)$, i.e.:
   $$u_3 = v_3 - \frac{\langle v_3, v_1 \rangle}{\langle v_1, v_1 \rangle} v_1 - \frac{\langle v_3, u_2 \rangle}{\langle u_2, u_2 \rangle} u_2$$

**Lemma 3.2.2** The constructed $\{u_1, u_2, u_3\}$ is an orthogonal set, i.e., $\langle u_i, u_j \rangle = 0$ when $i \neq j$.

**Proof**:

1. **Verify $\langle u_1, u_2 \rangle = 0$**:
   $$\langle u_1, u_2 \rangle = \langle v_1, v_2 - \frac{\langle v_2, v_1 \rangle}{\langle v_1, v_1 \rangle} v_1 \rangle$$
   $$= \langle v_1, v_2 \rangle - \frac{\langle v_2, v_1 \rangle}{\langle v_1, v_1 \rangle} \langle v_1, v_1 \rangle$$
   $$= \langle v_1, v_2 \rangle - \langle v_2, v_1 \rangle = 0$$

2. **Verify $\langle u_1, u_3 \rangle = 0$**:
   $$\langle u_1, u_3 \rangle = \langle v_1, v_3 - \text{proj}_{u_1}(v_3) - \text{proj}_{u_2}(v_3) \rangle$$
   $$= \langle v_1, v_3 \rangle - \langle v_1, \text{proj}_{u_1}(v_3) \rangle - \langle v_1, \text{proj}_{u_2}(v_3) \rangle$$

   Since $\text{proj}_{u_1}(v_3) = \frac{\langle v_3, v_1 \rangle}{\langle v_1, v_1 \rangle} v_1$, we have:
   $$\langle v_1, \text{proj}_{u_1}(v_3) \rangle = \frac{\langle v_3, v_1 \rangle}{\langle v_1, v_1 \rangle} \langle v_1, v_1 \rangle = \langle v_1, v_3 \rangle$$

   Since $\text{proj}_{u_2}(v_3)$ is orthogonal to $u_1$ (we have proved $\langle u_1, u_2 \rangle = 0$), we have:
   $$\langle v_1, \text{proj}_{u_2}(v_3) \rangle = 0$$

   Therefore:
   $$\langle u_1, u_3 \rangle = \langle v_1, v_3 \rangle - \langle v_1, v_3 \rangle - 0 = 0$$

3. **Verify $\langle u_2, u_3 \rangle = 0$**:
   $$\langle u_2, u_3 \rangle = \langle u_2, v_3 - \text{proj}_{u_1}(v_3) - \text{proj}_{u_2}(v_3) \rangle$$
   $$= \langle u_2, v_3 \rangle - \langle u_2, \text{proj}_{u_1}(v_3) \rangle - \langle u_2, \text{proj}_{u_2}(v_3) \rangle$$

   Since $\text{proj}_{u_1}(v_3)$ is orthogonal to $u_2$ (we have proved $\langle u_1, u_2 \rangle = 0$), we have:
   $$\langle u_2, \text{proj}_{u_1}(v_3) \rangle = 0$$

   Since $\text{proj}_{u_2}(v_3) = \frac{\langle v_3, u_2 \rangle}{\langle u_2, u_2 \rangle} u_2$, we have:
   $$\langle u_2, \text{proj}_{u_2}(v_3) \rangle = \frac{\langle v_3, u_2 \rangle}{\langle u_2, u_2 \rangle} \langle u_2, u_2 \rangle = \langle u_2, v_3 \rangle$$

   Therefore:
   $$\langle u_2, u_3 \rangle = \langle u_2, v_3 \rangle - 0 - \langle u_2, v_3 \rangle = 0$$

Therefore, $\{u_1, u_2, u_3\}$ is an orthogonal set.

### 3.3 Normalization of Orthogonal Basis

**Definition 3.3.1** Let $\{u_1, u_2, u_3\}$ be an orthogonal set, define the orthonormal basis $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$ as follows:

$$e_i = \frac{u_i}{\|u_i\|}, \quad i = 1, 2, 3$$

where $\|u\| = \sqrt{\langle u, u \rangle}$ is the norm of vector $u$.

**Theorem 3.3.1** $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$ is an orthonormal basis, satisfying:
- $\langle e_i, e_j \rangle = \delta_{ij}$ (orthogonality)
- $\|e_i\| = 1$ (normalization)

**Proof**: Directly from the properties of orthogonal set and normalization definition.

## 4. Orthogonal Combination Representation of Transcendental Irrational Numbers

### 4.1 Existence Theorem of Representation

**Theorem 4.1.1** For any transcendental irrational number $\alpha$, there exists an orthonormal basis $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$ (where $e_1, e_2$ are linear combinations of fractal dimensions, $e_3$ is a linear combination of rational numbers) and rational numbers $\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3$ such that:

$$\alpha = \lambda_1 e_1 + \lambda_2 e_2 + \lambda_3 e_3$$

**Proof**:

1. **Denseness of fractal dimensions**: According to Lemma 2.3.1 of module M-0.1, the fractal dimension set $\mathcal{D}$ is dense on the real number line $\mathbb{R}$. Therefore, for any transcendental irrational number $\alpha$, there exists a sequence of fractal dimensions $\{d_n\} \subset \mathcal{D}$ converging to $\alpha$.

2. **Completeness of fractal dimension space**: From the definition of fractal dimension space $V$, $\alpha$ can be represented as the limit of elements in $V$:
   $$\alpha = \lim_{n \to \infty} x_n$$

   where $x_n \in V$.

3. **Construction of orthogonal basis**: According to Theorem 3.1.1, there exists a linearly independent set $\{d_1, d_2, 1\}$. Through Schmidt orthogonalization (Theorem 3.2.1), construct an orthonormal basis $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$.

4. **Existence of representation**: For each $x_n$, since $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$ is an orthonormal basis, there exist rational numbers $\lambda_{n1}, \lambda_{n2}, \lambda_{n3}$ such that:
   $$x_n = \lambda_{n1} e_1 + \lambda_{n2} e_2 + \lambda_{n3} e_3$$

5. **Convergence of limit**: Since $\alpha$ is a transcendental irrational number and $V$ is a finite-dimensional space, its orthogonal basis representation $\alpha = \lambda_1 e_1 + \lambda_2 e_2 + \lambda_3 e_3$ is unique (Theorem 4.2.1). Therefore, for any approximation sequence $\{x_n\} \subset V$ converging to $\alpha$, the sequence of representation coefficients $\{\lambda_{ni}\}$ must converge to $\{\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3\}$.

### 4.2 Uniqueness of Representation

**Theorem 4.2.1 (Uniqueness Theorem)** The orthogonal combination representation of a transcendental irrational number is unique under the orthonormal basis.

**Proof**:

1. Suppose there exist two different representations:
   $$\alpha = \lambda_1 e_1 + \lambda_2 e_2 + \lambda_3 e_3 = \mu_1 e_1 + \mu_2 e_2 + \mu_3 e_3$$

2. Subtracting the two equations gives:
   $$(\lambda_1 - \mu_1) e_1 + (\lambda_2 - \mu_2) e_2 + (\lambda_3 - \mu_3) e_3 = 0$$

3. Since $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$ is an orthonormal basis, it is linearly independent, therefore:
   $$\lambda_1 - \mu_1 = \lambda_2 - \mu_2 = \lambda_3 - \mu_3 = 0$$

4. That is, $\lambda_i = \mu_i$ ($i=1,2,3$), so the representation is unique.

### 4.3 Properties of Orthogonal Combination Representation

**Theorem 4.3.1 (Least Square Property)** The orthogonal combination representation of a transcendental irrational number satisfies the least square property, i.e., for any orthonormal basis $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$, the representation coefficients $\lambda_i$ minimize $\|\alpha - \sum_{i=1}^3 \lambda_i e_i\|$.

**Proof**:

1. For any rational numbers $\mu_1, \mu_2, \mu_3$, consider the error vector:
   $$\epsilon = \alpha - \sum_{i=1}^3 \mu_i e_i$$

2. Expand $\alpha$ in the orthonormal basis:
   $$\alpha = \sum_{i=1}^3 \lambda_i e_i + \eta$$

   where $\eta$ is orthogonal to the subspace spanned by $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$.

3. Then:
   $$\|\epsilon\|^2 = \left\| \sum_{i=1}^3 (\lambda_i - \mu_i) e_i + \eta \right\|^2 = \sum_{i=1}^3 (\lambda_i - \mu_i)^2 + \|\eta\|^2$$

4. This is minimized when $\mu_i = \lambda_i$ for all $i$, giving $\|\epsilon\|^2 = \|\eta\|^2$.

5. Therefore, the representation coefficients $\lambda_i$ minimize the error norm.

## 5. Parameter Optimization Theory and Algorithm

### 5.1 Proposition of Parameter Optimization Problem

The parameter optimization problem in orthogonal combination representation is to find the optimal fractal dimensions and representation coefficients such that the representation error of transcendental irrational numbers is minimized.

**Definition 5.1.1** (Parameter Optimization Problem) Given a transcendental irrational number $\alpha$, find fractal dimensions $d_1, d_2$ and rational coefficients $q_1, q_2, r$ such that:

$$\min_{d_1, d_2, q_1, q_2, r} \left| \alpha - (q_1 d_1 + q_2 d_2 + r) \right|$$

### 5.2 L-BFGS-B Optimization Algorithm

The L-BFGS-B algorithm is a limited-memory version of the Broyden-Fletcher-Goldfarb-Shanno (BFGS) algorithm, which is suitable for large-scale optimization problems with bound constraints.

**Algorithm 5.2.1** (L-BFGS-B Algorithm for Parameter Optimization)

**Input:** Transcendental irrational number $\alpha$, initial fractal dimensions $d_1^0, d_2^0$, initial coefficients $q_1^0, q_2^0, r^0$, convergence tolerance $\epsilon$.

**Output:** Optimized fractal dimensions $d_1^*, d_2^*$ and coefficients $q_1^*, q_2^*, r^*$.

**Steps:**
1. Initialize the L-BFGS-B optimizer with the initial parameters.
2. Define the objective function as the absolute error $\left| \alpha - (q_1 d_1 + q_2 d_2 + r) \right|$.
3. Set bound constraints for fractal dimensions (positive real numbers) and coefficients (rational numbers).
4. Run the L-BFGS-B algorithm to minimize the objective function.
5. Return the optimized parameters.

### 5.3 Exploration of Fractal Dimension Space

To efficiently explore the fractal dimension space, we use a combination of grid search and random sampling:

**Algorithm 5.3.1** (Fractal Dimension Space Exploration)

**Input:** Search range for fractal dimensions, number of samples $N$.

**Output:** Set of candidate fractal dimensions.

**Steps:**
1. Generate a grid of fractal dimensions within the search range.
2. Generate random fractal dimensions using a uniform distribution.
3. Combine the grid points and random samples to form the candidate set.
4. Evaluate each candidate by its ability to represent transcendental irrational numbers.
5. Select the top-performing candidates for further optimization.

### 5.4 Dynamic Verification Mechanism

A dynamic verification mechanism is established to ensure the accuracy and reliability of the representation:

**Algorithm 5.4.1** (Dynamic Verification Mechanism)

**Input:** Representation parameters $d_1, d_2, q_1, q_2, r$, verification threshold $\tau$.

**Output:** Verification result (pass/fail).

**Steps:**
1. Calculate the representation error $\epsilon = \left| \alpha - (q_1 d_1 + q_2 d_2 + r) \right|$.
2. If $\epsilon < \tau$, the verification passes; otherwise, it fails.

## 7. Computational Complexity Analysis

### 7.1 Construction Complexity

The construction complexity of the orthogonal basis is analyzed as follows:

**Theorem 7.1.1** The computational complexity of constructing an n-dimensional orthogonal basis using Schmidt orthogonalization is $O(n^3)$.

**Proof**:

For each vector in the orthogonalization process:
- Calculating the projection onto each previously constructed orthogonal vector takes $O(n)$ operations.
- There are $n$ vectors to orthogonalize.
- For each vector, there are $n-1$ projections to calculate.

Total operations: $O(n^2 \times n) = O(n^3)$.

### 7.2 Representation Complexity

The representation complexity of transcendental irrational numbers is analyzed as follows:

**Theorem 7.2.1** The computational complexity of representing a transcendental irrational number using orthogonal combination is $O(n^2)$.

**Proof**:

To represent a transcendental irrational number in an n-dimensional orthonormal basis:
- Calculating each representation coefficient takes $O(n)$ operations.
- There are $n$ coefficients to calculate.

Total operations: $O(n \times n) = O(n^2)$.
