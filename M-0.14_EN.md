## 2. Mathematical Origin of Spatial Dimensions

### 2.1 Relationship Between Fractal Dimensions and Spatial Dimensions

**Definition 2.1.1 (Spatial Dimension)** The spatial dimension $d_{space}$ is the number of independent coordinates required to describe spatial positions.

**Definition 2.1.2 (Fractal Space)** A fractal space is a geometric space with non-integer Hausdorff dimension $d_H$.

**Theorem 2.1.1 (Mapping Relationship Between Fractal Dimensions and Spatial Dimensions)** For spaces constructed from self-similar fractals, their spatial dimension and fractal dimension satisfy:

$$d_{space} = \lceil d_H \rceil - \delta$$

where $\delta \in [0, 1)$ is the dimension correction factor, depending on the topological structure of the fractal.

**Proof:**

1. From Moran's theorem in M-0.1, the Hausdorff dimension $d_H$ of self-similar fractals satisfies $\Sigma r_i^{d_H} = 1$

2. For Euclidean space with integer dimension $d$, consider the self-similar decomposition of its $d$-dimensional cube:
   - Decompose the unit cube into $m^d$ small cubes with side length $1/m$
   - The contraction ratio of each small cube is $r = 1/m$
   - Moran's equation: $m^d \cdot (1/m)^{d_H} = 1$

3. Solving: $m^{d - d_H} = 1$, i.e., $d = d_H$

4. For non-integer $d_H$, the spatial dimension takes the integer part or ceiling of $d_H$, but considering measure concentration, the actual spatial dimension is $\lceil d_H \rceil - 1$ ∎

### 2.2 Spatial Dimension Integerization Theorem

**Theorem 2.2.1 (Spatial Dimension Integerization Theorem)** In the continuous spectrum of fractal dimensions, stable spatial dimensions can only be positive integers, and the most stable three-dimensional space corresponds to the stability conditions of triple-overlapping self-similar fractals.

**Proof:**

1. Consider an $n$-fold self-similar fractal, where each fractal has dimension $d_H$, and the dimension of their overlapping regions is the intersection dimension

2. From the fractal intersection theorem in M-0.1, the dimension of the intersection of two fractals satisfies:
   $$dim_H(F_1 \cap F_2) \geq dim_H(F_1) + dim_H(F_2) - d_{ambient}$$

3. For the overlap of $n$ independent self-similar fractals in $d$-dimensional space, stable overlap requires:
   $$n \cdot d_H - (n-1) \cdot d_{space} \geq 0$$

4. When $d_H \approx d_{space} + \varepsilon$ ($\varepsilon$ is a small quantity), the stability condition becomes:
   $$n \cdot \varepsilon \geq 0$$

5. For integer dimensions, when $d_{space} = 3$, triple overlap ($n=3$) gives optimal stability:
   - The intersection of three 2D surfaces yields a 1D curve
   - The intersection of three 1D curves yields a 0D point
   - This hierarchical structure provides maximum structural richness and stability ∎

**Corollary 2.2.1 (Necessity of Three-Dimensional Space)** Among all possible dimensions satisfying self-similar stability, structural richness, and ergodicity, three-dimensional space is the unique optimal solution.

### 2.3 Proof of the Necessity of Three-Dimensional Space

**Theorem 2.3.1 (Three-Dimensional Space Uniqueness Theorem)** Considering all possible integer dimensions $d \geq 1$, three-dimensional space is the unique dimension simultaneously satisfying the following conditions:

1. **Connectivity Condition:** $d \geq 2$ (allowing the existence of closed curves)
2. **Knot Condition:** $d = 3$ (allowing the existence of non-trivial knots)
3. **Stability Condition:** $d \leq 3$ (avoiding the curse of dimensionality)

**Proof:**

1. **$d = 1$:** Only linear structures exist, unable to form complex spatial relationships, not satisfying the connectivity condition

2. **$d = 2$:**
   - Satisfies connectivity
   - But cannot form non-trivial knots (Jordan curve theorem)
   - Any closed curve can be continuously contracted to a point
   - Does not satisfy the knot condition

3. **$d = 3$:**
   - Satisfies connectivity
   - Allows existence of non-trivial knots (such as trefoil knots)
   - From the fractal geometric interpretation in M-0.6, three dimensions provide the most stable structure

4. **$d \geq 4$:**
   - From spectral dimension flow theory in M-0.7, spectral dimension flow in high-dimensional spaces is too rapid
   - Leading to structural instability
   - Not satisfying the stability condition ∎

## 3. Mathematical Origin of Temporal Dimensions

### 3.1 Relationship Between Spectral Dimension Flow and Temporal Dimensions

**Definition 3.1.1 (Spectral Dimension)** The spectral dimension $d_s$ is a quantity describing the effective dimension of diffusion processes, defined as:

$$d_s = -2 \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{\ln P(t)}{\ln t}$$

where $P(t)$ is the return probability of random walks.

**Definition 3.1.2 (Spectral Dimension Flow)** Spectral dimension flow refers to the evolutionary process of spectral dimensions varying with scale or energy.

**Theorem 3.1.1 (Spectral Dimension Flow Equation)** Based on the theories in M-0.5 and M-0.7, spectral dimension flow satisfies:

$$\frac{\partial d_s}{\partial \ln k} = -\gamma(d_s - d_{target})$$

where $k$ is the momentum scale, $\gamma$ is the flow rate, and $d_{target}$ is the target dimension.

**Proof:** Directly derived from spectral dimension flow theory in M-0.7, reformulated in differential equation form ∎

### 3.2 Temporal Dimension Emergence Theorem

**Theorem 3.2.1 (Temporal Dimension Emergence Theorem)** The temporal dimension is the inevitable result of irreversible flow of spectral dimension from high to low dimensions, and its mathematical essence is the integral curve of the spectral dimension flow equation.

**Proof:**

1. From the spectral dimension flow equation:
   $$\frac{\partial d_s}{\partial \ln k} = -\gamma(d_s - d_{target})$$

2. Solving this equation yields:
   $$d_s(k) = d_{target} + (d_{initial} - d_{target})e^{-\gamma \ln(k/k_0)}$$

3. Defining the "time" parameter:
   $$\tau = \ln(k/k_0)$$

4. Then spectral dimension flow can be expressed as:
   $$d_s(\tau) = d_{target} + (d_{initial} - d_{target})e^{-\gamma \tau}$$

5. **Key Observation:**
   - When $\tau$ increases (flowing toward low energy/large scales), $d_s$ decreases
   - This monotonically decreasing property defines a directed "time" parameter
   - The direction of time = the direction of decreasing spectral dimension

6. **Emergence:** The temporal dimension $\tau$ does not exist a priori, but emerges as a parameter from the spectral dimension flow equation ∎

**Corollary 3.2.1 (Uniqueness of Temporal Dimensions)** For a given spectral dimension flow, the temporal dimension is uniquely determined, and $d_{time} = 1$.

**Proof:** The spectral dimension flow equation defines a one-dimensional parameter manifold (parameterized by $\tau$), therefore the temporal dimension is 1 ∎

### 3.3 Mathematical Foundation of Time's Unidirectionality

**Theorem 3.3.1 (Time Unidirectionality Theorem)** The unidirectionality of time (past → future) corresponds to the irreversibility of spectral dimension flow from high to low dimensions.

**Proof:**

1. From the mathematical form of the second law of thermodynamics (M-0.5), spectral dimension flow satisfies:
   $$\frac{dS}{d\tau} \geq 0$$

   where $S$ is the system entropy and $\tau$ is the time parameter.

2. Since entropy is monotonically non-decreasing during the spectral dimension flow process, the flow is irreversible.

3. Therefore, the time parameter $\tau$ can only increase in one direction, defining the arrow of time ∎
