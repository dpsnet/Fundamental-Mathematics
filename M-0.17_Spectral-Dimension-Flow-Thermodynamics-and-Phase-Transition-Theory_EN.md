## 1. Spectral Dimension Thermodynamics Zeroth Law

### 1.1 Problem Statement

The classical thermodynamics zeroth law allows us to define temperature as an indicator of thermal equilibrium. However, when systems are at different effective dimensions:
- System A: $d_s^{(A)} = 4$ (classical spacetime)
- System B: $d_s^{(B)} = 2$ (fractal core)

Does the traditional temperature definition still apply? Can systems at different dimensions reach thermal equilibrium?

### 1.2 Spectral Dimension Entropy and Spectral Dimension Temperature

**Definition (Spectral Dimension Entropy):** In $d_s$-dimensional effective spacetime, entropy is a function of energy and dimension:
$$S(E, d_s) = k_B \left[\alpha(d_s) \ln\frac{E}{E_0} + \frac{d_s}{3}\ln\frac{V}{V_0}\right]$$

where $\alpha(d_s)$ is the spectral dimension-dependent energy exponent.

**Definition (Spectral Dimension Temperature):**
$$\boxed{\frac{1}{T(d_s)} = \left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial E}\right)_{V, d_s} = \frac{k_B \alpha(d_s)}{E}}$$

That is:
$$T(d_s) = \frac{E}{k_B \alpha(d_s)}$$

**Key Insight:** Systems with the same energy $E$ have **different temperatures** at different effective dimensions!

### 1.3 Complete Statement of the Spectral Dimension Zeroth Law

> **Theorem 1.1 (Spectral Dimension Zeroth Law):** Two systems reach thermal equilibrium if and only if:
> 1. **Isothermal condition:** $T_A = T_B$ (spectral dimension temperatures are equal)
> 2. **Iso-dimensional condition:** $\mu_d^{(A)} = \mu_d^{(B)}$ (inter-dimensional chemical potentials are equal)
>
> where the inter-dimensional chemical potential is defined as:
> $$\mu_d = -T\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial d_s}\right)_{E,V} = -k_B T \left[\alpha'(d_s)\ln\frac{E}{E_0} + \frac{1}{3}\ln\frac{V}{V_0}\right]$$

### 1.4 Spectral Dimension Interpretation of Black Hole Thermal Equilibrium

Consider a black hole-thermal bath system:
- Thermal bath ($d_s = 4$): $T_{\text{bath}} = T_0$
- Horizon ($d_s = 3$): $T_{\text{horizon}} = T_0 \cdot \frac{\alpha(4)}{\alpha(3)}$
- Interior ($d_s = 2$): $T_{\text{interior}} = T_0 \cdot \frac{\alpha(4)}{\alpha(2)}$

**Conclusion:** The "temperature" measured by the black hole interior and exterior observers is different, but this is a normal phenomenon of spectral dimension dependence, not thermal disequilibrium!
