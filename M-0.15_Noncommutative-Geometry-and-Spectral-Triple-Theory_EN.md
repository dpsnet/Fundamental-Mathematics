## 2. Construction of Spectral Triples

### 2.1 Construction of Algebra $\mathcal{A}$

**Definition 2.1.1 (Fractal Algebra)** Let $M$ be a fractal spacetime manifold, define algebra $\mathcal{A}$ as the algebra of smooth functions on $M$:

$$\mathcal{A} = C^\infty(M) = \{f: M \to \mathbb{C} \mid f \text{ smooth}\}$$

On fractal spacetime, "smooth" requires special definition. Due to the singularity of fractal structure, we use **fractional Sobolev spaces**:

**Definition 2.1.2 (Fractional Smoothness)** A function $f$ is called $s$-smooth on fractal spacetime if:

$$\|f\|_{H^s}^2 = \int_M |(-\Delta)^{s/2}f|^2 d\mu_H < \infty$$

where $\Delta$ is the fractal Laplacian and $\mu_H$ is the Hausdorff measure.

**Theorem 2.1.1 (Completeness of Algebra)** Algebra $\mathcal{A}$ forms a Banach algebra on fractal spacetime with respect to pointwise multiplication and norm $\|\cdot\|_{H^s}$.

**Proof:**
1. Closedness: By Sobolev embedding theorem, $H^s$ is closed under pointwise multiplication
2. Associativity: Associativity of function multiplication
3. Identity: Constant function $1 \in \mathcal{A}$
4. Completeness: $H^s$ is a Hilbert space, hence complete

$\square$

**Definition 2.1.3 (Smooth Subalgebra)** The smooth subalgebra $\mathcal{A}_\infty \subset \mathcal{A}$ is defined as:

$$\mathcal{A}_\infty = \bigcap_{s>0} H^s(M)$$

This is a dense subalgebra of $\mathcal{A}$, corresponding to "infinitely differentiable" functions.

### 2.2 Completion of Hilbert Space $\mathcal{H}$

**Definition 2.2.1 (Spinor Bundle)** Define the spinor bundle $S \to M$ on fractal spacetime $M$, with fiber being the spinor space $S_x \cong \mathbb{C}^{2^n}$ ($n$ is the spatial dimension).

**Definition 2.2.2 (Hilbert Space)** Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$ is defined as the $L^2$ space of spinor fields:

$$\mathcal{H} = L^2(M, S) = \{\psi: M \to S \mid \int_M \|\psi\|^2 d\mu_H < \infty\}$$

The inner product is defined as:

$$(\psi, \phi) = \int_M \psi^\dagger(x)\phi(x) d\mu_H(x)$$

**Theorem 2.2.1 (Completeness of Hilbert Space)** $(\mathcal{H}, (\cdot, \cdot))$ forms a Hilbert space.

**Proof:** Directly from Lebesgue integration theory and completeness of $L^2$ space. The regularity of fractal measure $\mu_H$ guarantees the well-definedness of integration. $\square$

**Note 2.2.1** On fractal spacetime, the measure $\mu_H$ is a singular measure, different from ordinary Lebesgue measure. This leads to special structures of the Hilbert space.

### 2.3 Definition of Dirac Operator $D$

**Definition 2.3.1 (Fractal Dirac Operator)** On fractal spacetime, the Dirac operator $D$ is defined as:

$$D = \gamma^\mu \nabla_\mu + \tau_{\mu\nu}\gamma^{[\mu}\gamma^{\nu]}$$

where:
- $\gamma^\mu$ are Clifford algebra generators
- $\nabla_\mu$ is the covariant derivative (including Levi-Civita connection and torsion connection)
- $\tau_{\mu\nu}$ is the torsion tensor

**Theorem 2.3.1 (Self-adjointness of Dirac Operator)** The Dirac operator $D$ is self-adjoint on domain $\text{Dom}(D) = \{\psi \in \mathcal{H} : D\psi \in \mathcal{H}\}$.

**Proof:**
1. Symmetry: For $\psi, \phi \in \text{Dom}(D)$
   $$(\psi, D\phi) = (D\psi, \phi) + \text{boundary terms}$$
   On compact manifolds or with appropriate boundary conditions, boundary terms vanish.

2. Self-adjointness: By von Neumann theorem, self-adjoint extension of symmetric operator exists and is unique (under appropriate conditions).

$\square$

**Theorem 2.3.2 (Compact Resolvent)** The resolvent $(D - \lambda)^{-1}$ of Dirac operator $D$ is a compact operator for $\lambda \notin \text{Spec}(D)$.

**Proof:** This is equivalent to proving that $D$ has purely discrete spectrum. On compact fractal manifolds, the spectrum of Dirac operator is discrete, and eigenvalues tend to infinity. $\square$

### 2.4 Axioms of Spectral Triples

**Definition 2.4.1 (Spectral Triple)** A triple $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{H}, D)$ is called a spectral triple if it satisfies the following axioms:

**Axiom 1 (Dimension)** There exists $p \in \mathbb{N}$ (called spectral dimension) such that $|D|^{-p} \in \mathcal{L}^{1,\infty}$ (weak trace class).

**Axiom 2 (Regularity)** For any $a \in \mathcal{A}$, $[D, a]$ is a bounded operator on $\mathcal{H}$.

**Axiom 3 (Finite Summability)** For any $a \in \mathcal{A}$, $a|D|^{-s}$ is trace class for $\text{Re}(s) > p$.

**Axiom 4 (Real Structure)** There exists an anti-linear isometry $J: \mathcal{H} \to \mathcal{H}$, satisfying:
- $J^2 = \pm 1$
- $JD = \pm DJ$
- $JaJ^{-1}$ commutes (or anticommutes) with $\mathcal{A}$

**Theorem 2.4.1 (Existence of Spectral Triple on Fractal Spacetime)** On fractal spacetime $M$, $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{H}, D)$ forms a spectral triple satisfying all the above axioms.

**Proof Sketch:**
1. Dimension axiom: From heat kernel asymptotic $K(t) \sim t^{-d_s/2}$, take $p = d_s$.
2. Regularity: For $a \in \mathcal{A}$, $[D, a] = \gamma^\mu \partial_\mu a$ is a bounded operator.
3. Finite summability: From Weyl asymptotic formula $N(\lambda) \sim \lambda^{d_s/2}$.
4. Real structure: Take charge conjugation operator as $J$.

$\square$

## 3. Rigorous Theory of Connes Metric

### 3.1 Connes Distance Formula

**Definition 3.1.1 (Connes Metric)** For $p, q \in M$, define Connes distance:

$$d_C(p,q) = \sup\{|a(p)-a(q)| : a \in \mathcal{A}, \|[D,a]\| \leq 1\}$$

**Theorem 3.1.1 (Well-definedness of Distance)** $d_C(p,q)$ is a metric on $M$, satisfying:
- Positivity: $d_C(p,q) \geq 0$, and $d_C(p,q) = 0 \Leftrightarrow p = q$
- Symmetry: $d_C(p,q) = d_C(q,p)$
- Triangle inequality: $d_C(p,r) \leq d_C(p,q) + d_C(q,r)$

**Proof:**
1. Positivity: If $p \neq q$, one can take $a$ as a function taking different values at $p$ and $q$.
2. Symmetry: Directly from definition.
3. Triangle inequality: For any $a$ satisfying $\|[D,a]\| \leq 1$:
   $$|a(p)-a(r)| \leq |a(p)-a(q)| + |a(q)-a(r)| \leq d_C(p,q) + d_C(q,r)$$
   Taking supremum gives the result.

$\square$

### 3.2 Equivalence with Riemannian Metric

**Theorem 3.2.1 (Metric Equivalence)** On smooth Riemannian manifolds, Connes metric coincides with Riemannian metric:

$$d_C(p,q) = d_g(p,q) = \inf_\gamma \int_0^1 \|\gamma'(t)\|_g dt$$

where the infimum is taken over all piecewise smooth curves connecting $p$ and $q$.

**Proof:**

**Step 1**: Prove $d_C \leq d_g$

For any $a \in \mathcal{A}$ satisfying $\|[D,a]\| \leq 1$, along curve $\gamma$:
$$|a(p)-a(q)| = \left|\int_0^1 \frac{d}{dt}a(\gamma(t))dt\right| = \left|\int_0^1 (da)(\gamma'(t))dt\right|$$

Since $[D,a] = \gamma^\mu \partial_\mu a$, we have $|da| \leq \|[D,a]\| \leq 1$, therefore:
$$|a(p)-a(q)| \leq \int_0^1 \|\gamma'(t)\|_g dt = L(\gamma)$$

Taking infimum gives $d_C(p,q) \leq d_g(p,q)$.

**Step 2**: Prove $d_g \leq d_C$

Take $a(x) = d_g(x,p)$ (distance function), then $|\nabla a| = 1$ a.e., so $\|[D,a]\| = 1$.

Thus:
$$d_g(p,q) = |a(q)-a(p)| \leq d_C(p,q)$$

**Step 3**: Combine to get $d_C = d_g$

$\square$

### 3.3 Geometric Meaning of Fractal Correction Terms

On fractal spacetime, due to geometric singularities, Connes metric differs from Riemannian metric.

**Theorem 3.3.1 (Fractal Correction)** On fractal spacetime:

$$d_C(p,q) = d_g(p,q) + \delta_\tau(p,q)$$

where the fractal correction term is:
$$\delta_\tau(p,q) = c_\tau \cdot d_H(p,q)^{d_s-1} \cdot \tau_{\text{eff}}$$

- $d_H$ is the Hausdorff metric
- $c_\tau$ is a constant dependent on twist parameters
- $\tau_{\text{eff}}$ is effective twist strength

**Geometric Interpretation:**
1. **Short-distance behavior**: When $d_H \to 0$, if $d_s > 1$, correction term $\delta_\tau \to 0$, restoring classical geometry
2. **Long-distance behavior**: At macroscopic scales, $\delta_\tau$ can accumulate to produce observable effects
3. **Twist effect**: Stronger twist leads to larger corrections

## 4. Noncommutative Integration Theory

### 4.1 Rigorous Definition of Dixmier Trace

**Definition 4.1.1 (Weak Trace Class)** A compact operator $T$ belongs to weak trace class $\mathcal{L}^{1,\infty}$ if:

$$\sigma_N(T) = \sum_{n=1}^N \mu_n(T) = O(\log N)$$

where $\mu_n(T)$ is the $n$-th singular value of $T$ (in descending order).

**Definition 4.1.2 (Dixmier Trace)** For $T \in \mathcal{L}^{1,\infty}$, the Dixmier trace is defined as:

$$\text{Tr}_\omega(T) = \lim_{N\to\omega} \frac{1}{\log N} \sum_{n=1}^N \mu_n(T)$$

where $\lim_{N\to\omega}$ is the Dixmier limit (a special Banach limit).

**Theorem 4.1.1 (Properties of Dixmier Trace)** Dixmier trace satisfies:
1. Linearity: $\text{Tr}_\omega(aT + bS) = a\text{Tr}_\omega(T) + b\text{Tr}_\omega(S)$
2. Positivity: $T \geq 0 \Rightarrow \text{Tr}_\omega(T) \geq 0$
3. Unitary invariance: $\text{Tr}_\omega(UTU^*) = \text{Tr}_\omega(T)$
4. Ergodicity: For trace class operator $A$, $\text{Tr}_\omega(AT) = 0$

**Proof:** Directly from properties of Dixmier limit. $\square$

### 4.2 Correspondence with Fractal Measure

**Theorem 4.2.1 (Noncommutative Integration and Fractal Measure)** For $f \in \mathcal{A}$:

$$\text{Tr}_\omega(f|D|^{-d_s}) = c_{d_s} \int_M f d\mu_H$$

where $c_{d_s} = \frac{1}{d_s(2\pi)^{d_s}}$ is the normalization constant.

**Proof Sketch:**

**Step 1**: Heat kernel asymptotic
$$\text{Tr}(e^{-tD^2}) \sim \frac{1}{(4\pi t)^{d_s/2}} \text{Vol}(M)$$

**Step 2**: Singular value asymptotic
From Weyl law, $\mu_n(|D|^{-1}) \sim n^{-1/d_s}$

**Step 3**: Dixmier trace calculation
$$\text{Tr}_\omega(|D|^{-d_s}) = \lim_{N\to\omega} \frac{1}{\log N} \sum_{n=1}^N n^{-1} = 1$$

**Step 4**: Function multiplication
Using pseudodifferential operator calculus, relate the spectrum of $f|D|^{-d_s}$ to fractal measure.

$\square$

### 4.3 Spectral Action Principle

**Definition 4.3.1 (Spectral Action)** The spectral action is defined as:

$$S_\Lambda(D) = \text{Tr}(f(D^2/\Lambda^2))$$

where $f$ is a cutoff function and $\Lambda$ is the energy cutoff.

**Theorem 4.3.1 (Asymptotic Expansion of Spectral Action)** When $\Lambda \to \infty$:

$$S_\Lambda(D) \sim \sum_{k=0}^n f_k \Lambda^{d_s-k} a_k(D^2) + O(\Lambda^{d_s-n-1})$$

where:
- $f_k = \int_0^\infty f(u) u^{(d_s-k)/2-1} du$
- $a_k(D^2)$ are heat kernel coefficients (see M-2)

**Physical Interpretation:**
- $k=0$ term: Cosmological constant (proportional to volume)
- $k=2$ term: Einstein-Hilbert action
- $k=4$ term: Higher curvature corrections (Weyl squared, etc.)

## 5. Noncommutative Characterization of Spectral Dimension

### 5.1 Definition of Spectral Dimension from Noncommutative Geometry Perspective

**Definition 5.1.1 (Noncommutative Spectral Dimension)** The spectral dimension of spectral triple $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{H}, D)$ is defined as:

$$d_{s}^{NC} = \inf\{p > 0 : |D|^{-p} \in \mathcal{L}^1\}$$

Equivalently:

$$d_{s}^{NC} = -2 \lim_{t\to 0^+} \frac{\ln \text{Tr}(e^{-tD^2})}{\ln t}$$

### 5.2 Proof of Consistency with Heat Kernel Definition

**Theorem 5.2.1 (Definition Equivalence)** The noncommutative spectral dimension $d_s^{NC}$ is consistent with the spectral dimension $d_s$ defined by heat kernel in M-2.

**Proof:**

**Step 1**: Relation between heat kernel trace and spectral counting function
$$\text{Tr}(e^{-tD^2}) = \int_0^\infty e^{-t\lambda} dN(\lambda)$$

where $N(\lambda)$ is the spectral counting function.

**Step 2**: Weyl asymptotic
On fractal spacetime, $N(\lambda) \sim C \lambda^{d_s/2}$

**Step 3**: Asymptotic calculation
$$\text{Tr}(e^{-tD^2}) \sim C \int_0^\infty e^{-t\lambda} \lambda^{d_s/2-1} d\lambda = C' t^{-d_s/2}$$

**Step 4**: Take logarithm
$$\ln \text{Tr}(e^{-tD^2}) \sim -\frac{d_s}{2} \ln t + \text{const}$$

Therefore:
$$-2 \lim_{t\to 0} \frac{\ln \text{Tr}(e^{-tD^2})}{\ln t} = d_s$$

$\square$

### 5.3 Noncommutative Explanation of Spectral Dimension Flow

**Theorem 5.3.1 (Noncommutative Description of Spectral Dimension Flow)** Spectral dimension flow corresponds to deformation of the Dirac operator:

$$D_\mu = D + \mu \cdot \delta D$$

where $\mu$ is the energy scale and $\delta D$ is the twist correction.

The running of spectral dimension:
$$d_s(\mu) = d_s^{(0)} + \alpha \ln\frac{\mu}{\mu_0} + \beta \left(\ln\frac{\mu}{\mu_0}\right)^2 + \cdots$$

**Physical Interpretation:**
1. **High-energy limit** ($\mu \to \infty$): $d_s \to 2$, corresponding to UV fixed point
2. **Low-energy limit** ($\mu \to 0$): $d_s \to 4$, restoring classical spacetime
3. **Renormalization group flow**: Energy dependence of spectral dimension corresponds to scale evolution of the theory

## 6. Noncommutative Description of Multi-Twist

### 6.1 Algebraic Structure of Twist Operators

**Definition 6.1.1 (Twist Operator)** The twist operator $T$ is defined as:

$$T = \tau_{\mu\nu} \gamma^{[\mu}\gamma^{\nu]}$$

where $\tau_{\mu\nu}$ is the torsion tensor and $\gamma^{[\mu}\gamma^{\nu]} = \frac{1}{2}[\gamma^\mu, \gamma^\nu]$.

**Theorem 6.1.1 (Clifford Structure of Twist Algebra)** Twist operators generate a subalgebra of Clifford algebra:

$$\{T_a, T_b\} = 2\delta_{ab} \cdot \mathbf{1}$$

where $T_a$ are normalized twist operator bases.

### 6.2 Coupling between Twist and Spectral Dimension

**Theorem 6.2.1 (Twist-Spectral Dimension Coupling Equation)** Twist parameter $\tau$ and spectral dimension $d_s$ satisfy the coupling equation:

$$\frac{dd_s}{d\ln\mu} = \alpha (d_s - 4)(d_s - 2) + \beta \tau^2$$

**Physical Significance:**
1. When $\tau = 0$ (no twist), standard spectral dimension flow is restored
2. Twist enhances the running of spectral dimension ($\beta > 0$)
3. In UV region ($d_s \approx 2$), twist effects are most significant
