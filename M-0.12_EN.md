## 2. Basic Theory of Heat Equation and Heat Kernel

### 2.1 Well-Posed Problems for Heat Equation

**Definition 2.1.1** Let $(M, g)$ be a complete Riemannian manifold. The **heat equation** on $M$ is:
$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \Delta u$$
where $\Delta = \Delta_g$ is the Laplace-Beltrami operator, and $t > 0$ is the time parameter.

**Definition 2.1.2** **Cauchy Problem:** Given initial condition $u(0, x) = f(x)$, find the solution $u(t, x)$ of the heat equation.

**Definition 2.1.3** **Dirichlet Problem:** On a bounded domain $\Omega \subset M$, given boundary condition $u(t, x) = 0$ for $x \in \partial\Omega$.

**Theorem 2.1.1** (Uniqueness of Heat Equation Solutions) On bounded domains, the solutions to the Cauchy problem and Dirichlet problem of the heat equation are unique.

**Proof:** By the energy method, let $u_1$ and $u_2$ be two solutions, and let $w = u_1 - u_2$, then $w$ satisfies the homogeneous heat equation. Consider the energy:
$$E(t) = \int_\Omega w^2 d\mu$$
Computing:
$$\frac{dE}{dt} = 2\int_\Omega w \frac{\partial w}{\partial t} d\mu = 2\int_\Omega w \Delta w d\mu = -2\int_\Omega |\nabla w|^2 d\mu \leq 0$$
and $E(0) = 0$, hence $E(t) = 0$, i.e., $w \equiv 0$.

### 2.2 Definition and Construction of Heat Kernel

**Definition 2.2.1** The **fundamental solution** (or **heat kernel**) of the heat equation is a function $p(t, x, y)$ satisfying:
1. For fixed $y$, $\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = \Delta_x p$ (satisfies heat equation as a function of $x$)
2. $\lim_{t \to 0^+} p(t, x, y) = \delta_y(x)$ (initial condition is the Dirac delta function)

**Theorem 2.2.1** (Existence and Uniqueness of Heat Kernel) On complete Riemannian manifolds, the heat kernel exists and is unique.

**Example 2.2.1** (Euclidean Space) The heat kernel on $\mathbb{R}^n$ is the Gaussian kernel:
$$p(t, x, y) = \frac{1}{(4\pi t)^{n/2}} \exp\left(-\frac{|x-y|^2}{4t}\right)$$

**Example 2.2.2** (Circle) The heat kernel on $S^1$:
$$p(t, x, y) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi t}} \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} \exp\left(-\frac{(x-y+2\pi k)^2}{4t}\right)$$

### 2.3 Fundamental Properties of Heat Kernel

**Theorem 2.3.1** (Regularity of Heat Kernel) The heat kernel $p(t, x, y)$ is a $C^\infty$ function for $(t, x, y) \in (0, \infty) \times M \times M$.

**Theorem 2.3.2** (Semigroup Property) The heat kernel satisfies the Chapman-Kolmogorov equation:
$$p(t+s, x, y) = \int_M p(t, x, z) p(s, z, y) d\mu(z)$$

**Proof:** This reflects the superposition principle of heat equation solutions and also embodies the Markov property.

**Theorem 2.3.3** (Symmetry) The heat kernel is symmetric in the spatial variables:
$$p(t, x, y) = p(t, y, x)$$

**Theorem 2.3.4** (Positivity and Normalization) On compact manifolds:
$$p(t, x, y) > 0, \quad \int_M p(t, x, y) d\mu(y) = 1$$

**Theorem 2.3.5** (Gaussian Upper Bound Estimate) On complete Riemannian manifolds, if Ricci curvature is bounded below, then there exist constants $C, c > 0$ such that:
$$p(t, x, y) \leq \frac{C}{V(x, \sqrt{t})} \exp\left(-c \frac{d(x, y)^2}{t}\right)$$
where $V(x, r)$ is the volume of the geodesic ball of radius $r$.

### 2.4 Heat Semigroup and Functional Analysis

**Definition 2.4.1** The **heat semigroup** $\{e^{t\Delta}\}_{t \geq 0}$ is defined as:
$$(e^{t\Delta}f)(x) = \int_M p(t, x, y) f(y) d\mu(y)$$

**Theorem 2.4.1** The heat semigroup is a strongly continuous contraction semigroup on $L^2(M)$.

**Proof:**
- **Semigroup property**: $e^{(t+s)\Delta} = e^{t\Delta} e^{s\Delta}$ follows from the semigroup property of the heat kernel
- **Strong continuity**: $\lim_{t \to 0} e^{t\Delta}f = f$ in the $L^2$ sense
- **Contraction property**: $\|e^{t\Delta}f\|_{L^2} \leq \|f\|_{L^2}$ follows from the normalization and symmetry of the heat kernel

**Theorem 2.4.2** (Hille-Yosida Theorem) The generator of the heat semigroup is the self-adjoint extension of the Laplacian operator.

## 3. Laplacian Operator and Spectral Theory

### 3.1 Laplace-Beltrami Operator

**Definition 3.1.1** In local coordinates $(x^1, \ldots, x^n)$, the **Laplace-Beltrami operator** is:
$$\Delta f = \frac{1}{\sqrt{|g|}} \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\left(\sqrt{|g|} g^{ij} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x^j}\right)$$
where $g = \det(g_{ij})$, and $(g^{ij})$ is the inverse of the metric matrix.

**Theorem 3.1.1** The Laplace-Beltrami operator is self-adjoint and non-positive on $L^2(M, d\mu)$.

### 3.2 Spectral Decomposition Theorem

**Theorem 3.2.1** (Spectral Decomposition for Compact Manifolds) Let $M$ be a compact Riemannian manifold, then the Laplacian operator has discrete spectrum:
$$0 = \lambda_0 < \lambda_1 \leq \lambda_2 \leq \cdots \to +\infty$$
The corresponding eigenfunctions $\{\phi_k\}$ form an orthonormal basis of $L^2(M)$.

**Proof Sketch:**
1. The inverse of the Laplacian operator is a compact operator (by Rellich-Kondrachov compact embedding theorem)
2. The spectral theorem for compact self-adjoint operators gives discrete spectrum
3. The smallest eigenvalue $\lambda_0 = 0$ corresponds to constant functions

### 3.3 Eigenvalue Problem

**Definition 3.3.1** **Eigenvalue Problem:** Find $\lambda$ and non-zero function $\phi$ such that:
$$\Delta \phi + \lambda \phi = 0$$

**Theorem 3.3.1** (Courant-Fischer Minimax Principle) The $k$-th eigenvalue:
$$\lambda_k = \min_{L_{k+1}} \max_{\phi \in L_{k+1}, \phi \neq 0} \frac{\int_M |\nabla \phi|^2 d\mu}{\int_M \phi^2 d\mu}$$
where $L_{k+1}$ ranges over all $(k+1)$-dimensional subspaces.

### 3.4 Spectral Representation of Heat Kernel

**Theorem 3.4.1** (Spectral Expansion of Heat Kernel) On compact manifolds, the heat kernel has the spectral representation:
$$p(t, x, y) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} e^{-\lambda_k t} \phi_k(x) \overline{\phi_k(y)}$$

**Proof:** For fixed $y$, the function $f_y(x) = p(t, x, y)$ can be expanded as a series of eigenfunctions:
$$f_y(x) = \sum_k c_k(t, y) \phi_k(x)$$
By the heat equation and orthogonality, $c_k(t, y) = e^{-\lambda_k t} \overline{\phi_k(y)}$.

**Corollary 3.4.1** (Spectral Representation of Heat Kernel Trace)
$$Z(t) = \int_M p(t, x, x) d\mu(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} e^{-\lambda_k t}$$

## 4. Weyl Asymptotic Theory

### 4.1 Eigenvalue Counting Function

**Definition 4.1.1** The **eigenvalue counting function** $N(\lambda)$ is defined as the number of eigenvalues not exceeding $\lambda$ (counting multiplicities):
$$N(\lambda) = \#\{k : \lambda_k \leq \lambda\}$$

**Theorem 4.1.1** On compact Riemannian manifolds:
$$N(\lambda) = O(\lambda^{n/2})$$

### 4.2 Weyl Law

**Theorem 4.2.1** (Weyl Law) For $n$-dimensional compact Riemannian manifolds:
$$N(\lambda) \sim \frac{\omega_n}{(2\pi)^n} \text{Vol}(M) \lambda^{n/2}, \quad \lambda \to +\infty$$
where $\omega_n = \frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma(n/2 + 1)}$ is the volume of the unit ball in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

**Equivalent Form:**
$$\lim_{\lambda \to \infty} \frac{N(\lambda)}{\lambda^{n/2}} = \frac{\omega_n}{(2\pi)^n} \text{Vol}(M)$$

**Proof Sketch:**
1. Through symbol calculus of pseudodifferential operators
2. Or through the diagonalization principle and trace estimates of spectral projections
3. Heat kernel method: deriving from the asymptotic behavior of heat kernel trace

**Corollary 4.2.1** (Average Asymptotic of Eigenvalues)
$$\lambda_k \sim \left(\frac{(2\pi)^n}{\omega_n \text{Vol}(M)} k\right)^{2/n}, \quad k \to \infty$$

### 4.3 Second Term and Curvature

**Theorem 4.3.1** (Second Term Correction) For closed surfaces ($n=2$):
$$N(\lambda) = \frac{\text{Area}(M)}{4\pi} \lambda - \frac{\chi(M)}{6} + o(1)$$
where $\chi(M)$ is the Euler characteristic.

**Theorem 4.3.2** (Second Term in General Dimensions) Under certain conditions, the second term of Weyl's law is related to the average scalar curvature.

### 4.4 Higher-Dimensional Generalizations

**Theorem 4.4.1** (Fractal Generalization of Weyl Law) For certain fractal sets, there exists a similar asymptotic behavior of the counting function:
$$N(\lambda) \sim C \lambda^{d_s/2}$$
where $d_s$ is the spectral dimension rather than the Hausdorff dimension.

## 5. Minakshisundaram-Pleijel Expansion

### 5.1 Local Expansion of Heat Kernel

**Theorem 5.1.1** (Asymptotic Expansion of Diagonal Heat Kernel) For compact Riemannian manifolds, as $t \to 0^+$:
$$p(t, x, x) \sim \frac{1}{(4\pi t)^{n/2}} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a_k(x) t^k$$
where $a_k(x)$ are smooth functions.

**Theorem 5.1.2** (Estimate for Off-Diagonal Expansion) For $x \neq y$, as $t \to 0^+$:
$$p(t, x, y) = O(t^{-n/2} \exp(-d(x, y)^2 / 4t))$$

### 5.2 Asymptotic Expansion of Heat Kernel Trace

**Theorem 5.2.1** (Minakshisundaram-Pleijel Expansion) For compact Riemannian manifolds:
$$Z(t) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} e^{-\lambda_k t} \sim \frac{1}{(4\pi t)^{n/2}} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a_k t^k, \quad t \to 0^+$$
where $a_k = \int_M a_k(x) d\mu(x)$ are the global heat invariants.

**Proof:** Integrating the local expansion:
$$Z(t) = \int_M p(t, x, x) d\mu(x) \sim \int_M \frac{1}{(4\pi t)^{n/2}} \sum_k a_k(x) t^k d\mu(x)$$

### 5.3 Heat Invariants

**Definition 5.3.1** The **heat invariants** $a_k$ are the coefficients in the M-S expansion.

**Theorem 5.3.1** (First Few Heat Invariants)
- $a_0 = \text{Vol}(M)$ (volume)

**Theorem 5.3.2** (Geometric Meaning of Heat Invariants) Each $a_k$ is the integral of a local differential invariant of the metric.

### 5.4 Geometric Meaning of Coefficients

**Theorem 5.4.1** (Derivation of Weyl Law) The Weyl law can be derived from the M-S expansion through Laplace inverse transform.

## 6. Rigorous Theory of Spectral Dimension

### 6.1 Definition of Spectral Dimension

**Definition 6.1.1** (Classical Definition of Spectral Dimension) Let $Z(t)$ be the heat kernel trace. If there exists $d_s$ such that:
$$Z(t) \sim C t^{-d_s/2}, \quad t \to 0^+$$
then $d_s$ is called the **spectral dimension**.

**Equivalent Definition:**
$$d_s = -2 \lim_{t \to 0^+} \frac{\ln Z(t)}{\ln t}$$

**Theorem 6.1.1** (Spectral Dimension of Compact Manifolds) For $n$-dimensional compact Riemannian manifolds:
$$d_s = n$$

**Proof:** From the M-S expansion, $Z(t) \sim (4\pi)^{-n/2} \text{Vol}(M) t^{-n/2}$, hence $d_s = n$.

### 6.2 Calculation Methods for Spectral Dimension

**Method 1: Direct Method**
$$d_s = -2 \frac{d \ln Z(t)}{d \ln t}\bigg|_{t \to 0}$$

**Method 2: Walk Dimension Method**
For random walks on fractals, define the **walk dimension** $d_w$ and **fractal dimension** $d_f$:
$$d_s = \frac{2d_f}{d_w}$$

**Method 3: Effective Spectral Dimension**
Define the effective spectral dimension depending on scale:
$$d_s(t) = -2 \frac{\ln Z(t)}{\ln t}$$

### 6.3 Relationship Between Spectral Dimension and Geometric Dimension

**Theorem 6.3.1** (General Relationship) For metric measure spaces:
$$d_s \leq d_f$$
Equality holds if and only if the space is sufficiently "regular".

**Theorem 6.3.2** (Sierpinski Gasket) For the Sierpinski gasket:
- $d_f = \log 3 / \log 2 \approx 1.585$
- $d_w = \log 5 / \log 2 \approx 2.322$
- $d_s = 2d_f / d_w = 2\log 3 / \log 5 \approx 1.365$

### 6.4 Effective Spectral Dimension

**Definition 6.4.1** The **effective spectral dimension** $d_s(t)$ is defined as:
$$d_s(t) = -2 \frac{\ln Z(t)}{\ln t}$$

**Theorem 6.4.1** For compact manifolds, $d_s(t) \to n$ as $t \to 0$.

**Definition 6.4.2** In unified field theory, spectral dimension varies with energy scale $E$, defined as:
$$d_s(E) = d_s(t = \hbar / E)$$

## 9. Heat Kernel Trace and Spectral Dimension Flow

### 9.1 Definition of Heat Kernel Trace

**Definition 9.1.1** The **heat kernel trace** $Z(t)$ is defined as:
$$Z(t) = \text{Tr}(e^{-t\Delta}) = \int_M p(t, x, x) d\mu(x)$$

**Physical Meaning:** $Z(t)$ is the trace of the heat semigroup $e^{-t\Delta}$, which is the partition function in statistical physics.

### 9.2 Relationship Between Heat Kernel Trace and Spectral Dimension

**Theorem 9.2.1** (Characterization of Spectral Dimension by Heat Kernel Trace)
$$d_s = -2 \lim_{t \to 0^+} \frac{\ln Z(t)}{\ln t}$$

**Theorem 9.2.2** (Effective Spectral Dimension)
$$d_s(t) = -2 \frac{d \ln Z(t)}{d \ln t}$$

### 9.3 Scale Transformation and Spectral Dimension Flow

**Definition 9.3.1** Let the relationship between energy scale $E$ and time parameter $t$ be $t \sim 1/E$.

**Definition 9.3.2** The **spectral dimension flow** $d_s(E)$ is defined as:
$$d_s(E) = -2 \frac{\ln Z(1/E)}{\ln (1/E)} = 2 \frac{\ln Z(1/E)}{\ln E}$$

**Theorem 9.3.1** (Continuity of Spectral Dimension Flow) Under appropriate regularity conditions, $d_s(E)$ is a continuous function of $E$.

### 9.4 Applications in Unified Field Theory

**Theorem 9.4.1** (Spectral Dimension Flow in Unified Field Theory) Under the "Fixed 4D Topology + Dynamic Spectral Dimension" paradigm:
$$d_s(E) = \begin{cases} 2 + \frac{\alpha}{\ln(E/E_0)}, & E \to \infty \text{ (UV)} \\ 4, & E \to 0 \text{ (IR)} \end{cases}$$

**Physical Interpretation:**
- **UV region** (high energy): $d_s \to 2$, naturally avoiding UV divergences
- **IR region** (low energy): $d_s \to 4$, recovering classical 4D physics

$$\mu \frac{d d_s}{d\mu} = \alpha (d_s - 4)^2 (d_s - 2)$$
